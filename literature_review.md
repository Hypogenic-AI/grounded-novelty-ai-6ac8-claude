# Literature Review — Scientific Idea Generation with Grounded Novelty

**Hypothesis under investigation.** *Large language models generate better scientific ideas when the
notion of novelty is **grounded** — through deduction, induction, proposal writing, outcome imagination,
small experiments, or literature comparison — rather than relying on abstract or ungrounded prompts.*

This review synthesizes 20 papers (curated from 218 unique candidates returned by paper-finder + arXiv
search) that bear directly on (a) *generating* ideas/hypotheses with LLMs, (b) *grounding* that
generation in literature, data, or experiments, and (c) *evaluating* novelty. Per-paper structured notes
(method, datasets, baselines, metrics, results) are in the second half of this document; the raw catalog
is in `resources.md` and `papers/README.md`.

---

## 1. Research Area Overview

LLM-based scientific ideation has moved, in roughly two years (2023→2025), from "can an LLM emit a
plausible hypothesis from a prompt?" to "how do we *ground* and *evaluate* ideas so that novelty is real
and useful, not just surface-level?" Three threads have emerged, and the surveys in this set
(arXiv:2504.05496; the Evolving-Roles survey) organize the field the same way:

- **Theory-driven / literature-grounded generation.** Retrieve prior work and generate ideas grounded in
  it, explicitly optimizing or checking novelty against the corpus. Canonical: **SciMON** (iterative
  retrieve–compare–revise until "sufficiently novel"), **ResearchAgent**, **Chain-of-Ideas**, **Nova**,
  **MOOSE / MOOSE-Chem** (decompose `hypothesis = background + inspirations`), **HypER**, **Scideator**
  (facet recombination). Grounding modes: *literature-comparison*, *retrieval*, *deduction*.
- **Data-driven / inductive generation.** Induce explanatory hypotheses from labeled examples and judge
  them by *downstream predictive accuracy* — a grounded signal of usefulness. Canonical: **HypoGeniC**
  (Zhou & Tan), **open-domain MOOSE** (Yang et al.), **HypoBench**. Grounding modes: *induction*,
  *small-experiments / outcome-checking*.
- **Synergistic & agentic.** Combine the two: **Literature Meets Data** (HypoRefine + Union) shows
  literature and data are *complementary*; **SGA** closes the loop with a simulator
  (*outcome-imagination + small-experiments*); **Learning-to-Generate-Ideas** adds explicit
  novelty/feasibility control.

In parallel, an **evaluation** literature has grown because "is this novel?" prompts are unreliable: the
landmark human study **Si et al. 2024** (100+ NLP researchers), the **SchNovel / RAG-Novelty** benchmark,
**Literature-Grounded Novelty Assessment**, and idea/hypothesis benchmarks **IdeaBench**, **ResearchBench**,
**HypoBench**. The consistent message: *grounding the novelty judgment in retrieved related work beats
abstract LLM-as-judge prompting* — the evaluation-side analogue of the project hypothesis.

## 2. How the six grounding mechanisms appear in the literature

The hypothesis names six grounding strategies. Mapping them onto concrete, reusable instantiations:

| Grounding mechanism | What it means operationally | Best exemplars in this set |
|---|---|---|
| **Literature comparison** | Retrieve prior work; force the idea to differ from it / score novelty against it | SciMON (novelty-boosting loop), Chain-of-Ideas, Nova, RAG-Novelty, Literature-Grounded Novelty Assessment |
| **Induction** | Generate hypotheses *from data/examples*, refine by evidence | HypoGeniC, open-domain MOOSE, HypoBench, Literature Meets Data |
| **Proposal writing** | Produce a full structured proposal (problem→method→experiments), critiqued and revised | ResearchAgent, Si et al. agent, Scideator |
| **Outcome imagination** | Predict/imagine experimental outcomes before committing | SGA (simulation feedback), Chain-of-Ideas (anticipated trends), HypoGeniC reward (predicted accuracy) |
| **Small experiments** | Run a cheap empirical check and feed results back | HypoGeniC / Literature Meets Data (downstream-accuracy as the check), SGA (differentiable simulation), HypER |
| **Deduction / decomposition** | Decompose the discovery into sub-steps and reason structurally | MOOSE-Chem (`background + inspirations`), HypoRefine |

**Ungrounded baseline** is well-represented too: *zero-shot / few-shot "propose a novel idea" prompting*
(LLMs-are-Zero-Shot-Hypothesis-Proposers; the zero-shot arms of SciMON, MOOSE, HypoGeniC). Every grounded
method in this set is measured *against* such a prompt — so the hypothesis is directly testable with
existing baselines.

## 3. What the evidence says about the hypothesis (the through-line)

The papers provide repeated, quantitative support that **grounding helps**, with one important caveat:

- **SciMON**: zero-shot GPT-4 ideas are low-novelty/low-depth; adding inspiration retrieval (KG/semantic)
  and an *iterative novelty-boosting loop* (compare to k=20 nearest prior ideas, force "significantly
  different") raises human-judged novelty for **46–58%** of ideas and **never lowers it**.
- **MOOSE (open-domain, Yang et al.)**: novelty rises from **2.48 → 3.39** (expert-confirmed direction)
  as multi-step feedback grounding is added, *while validity stays ~3.9* — grounding raises novelty
  without sacrificing plausibility.
- **HypoGeniC (Zhou & Tan)**: inductive, data-grounded hypotheses beat few-shot prompting by
  **3.3–31.7%** accuracy and even beat fine-tuned RoBERTa/Llama-2 oracles trained on 1000 examples.
- **Literature Meets Data**: literature + data > few-shot by **8.97%**, > literature-alone by **15.75%**,
  > data-alone by **3.37%** (OOD); and grounded hypotheses improve *human* accuracy by up to **14.19%**.
- **HypoBench** (194 datasets, 12 domains): data-driven and literature+data methods dominate
  inference-only and zero-shot-generation baselines, especially OOD.
- **Evaluation side** — **RAG-Novelty** and **Literature-Grounded Novelty Assessment** (F1 0.79, κ 0.59,
  acc 0.81 vs. 0.68 zero-shot) both show grounding the *judgment* in retrieved papers beats ungrounded
  LLM-as-judge.

**The caveat (critical for experiment design).** *Novelty ≠ value.* Si et al. find AI ideas are judged
**more novel** (5.64 vs 4.84, p<0.01) but **slightly less feasible** than experts'; their follow-up
**Ideation–Execution Gap** shows that once ideas are actually executed, the LLM novelty advantage
*shrinks and rankings can flip*. SciMON shows grounding still leaves an **85%** gap to real paper ideas on
technical depth. **Implication:** the project should measure not just a novelty score but *grounded
usefulness* (downstream accuracy, ground-truth recovery, feasibility) — exactly what the "outcome
imagination / small experiments / literature comparison" grounding modes provide.

## 4. Common Methodologies

- **Retrieve-then-generate** (RAG over a paper corpus / KG): SciMON, ResearchAgent, Chain-of-Ideas, Nova,
  HypER, MOOSE-Chem.
- **Iterative refine-and-check loops**: compare to prior work and revise (SciMON novelty boosting), or
  update from data via a bandit reward (HypoGeniC), or agent–reviewer debate (ResearchAgent, Nova).
- **Decomposition**: `hypothesis = background + inspirations` (MOOSE-Chem); facet recombination
  (Scideator); literature∪data union (Literature Meets Data).
- **Closed-loop with experiments/simulation**: SGA bilevel optimization; downstream-accuracy as the
  empirical check (HypoGeniC/HypoBench/Literature Meets Data).
- **Controllable generation**: explicit novelty/feasibility dimensions (Learning-to-Generate-Ideas).

## 5. Standard Baselines (use these)

1. **Zero-shot prompting** — "propose a novel hypothesis for q." (ungrounded; the key control)
2. **Few-shot prompting** — a handful of demonstrations.
3. **Literature-only generation** — retrieve + generate, no data/experiment grounding.
4. **Data-only generation** — HypoGeniC (induction), no literature.
5. **Supervised oracle** (for data tasks) — fine-tuned RoBERTa / Llama as an upper-bound reference.
6. **Strong grounded systems** as toplines — SciMON, HypoRefine/Union, MOOSE-Chem.

## 6. Evaluation Metrics (use a mix; do not rely on a single novelty score)

- **Grounded utility**: downstream classification accuracy (IND + **OOD** for generalizability);
  human-decision-improvement; **Hypothesis/Feature Discovery Rate** and **Relationship Correctness**
  (HypoBench); rediscovery similarity to held-out ground-truth hypotheses (MOOSE-Chem).
- **Novelty / quality (human or LLM-judge)**: Novelty, Excitement, Feasibility, Expected-Effectiveness,
  Overall on 1–10 (Si et al.); Validness/Novelty/Helpfulness 1–5 (MOOSE); pairwise novelty (SchNovel).
- **Grounded novelty judging**: RAG-Novelty / Literature-Grounded assessment (retrieval-conditioned),
  reported with **Cohen's κ** for human agreement — preferred over bare LLM-as-judge.
- **Rigor**: blind review, multiple reviewers, Welch's t-test + Bonferroni, diversity/duplication rate
  (idea generators collapse to few unique ideas at scale — Si et al.).

## 7. Datasets in the Literature (and what we downloaded)

- **HypoGeniC / HypoBench tasks** (downloaded → `datasets/`): deceptive reviews, dreaddit (stress),
  AIGC detection (gptgc/llamagc), headline engagement, retweets, persuasive pairs, paper citations,
  journal; plus controllable **synthetic** tasks (election, admission, shoe, preference). HF-style JSON,
  shared across HypoGeniC, Literature Meets Data, and HypoBench → a single task vocabulary for ablations.
- **Hypothesis-agent literature** (downloaded): per-task PDFs + parsed papers for the literature-grounded
  conditions.
- **Idea-level benchmarks** (in cloned code / available): MOOSE-Chem (51 annotated 2024 chemistry papers),
  open-domain TOMATO (50 social-science papers), Si et al. ideation+review data, IdeaBench, ResearchBench,
  SchNovel (15k arXiv pairs).

## 8. Gaps and Opportunities (where this project can contribute)

1. **No head-to-head of grounding *mechanisms* under one protocol.** Each paper grounds *one* way and
   compares to ungrounded prompting; nobody has held task/model/metric fixed and ablated
   deduction vs. induction vs. proposal-writing vs. outcome-imagination vs. small-experiments vs.
   literature-comparison. **This is exactly the project's opening** — and the ChicagoHAI framework
   already implements the ungrounded baseline + induction + literature conditions on shared tasks.
2. **Novelty is over-trusted.** Given Si et al.'s ideation–execution flip, the project should report
   *grounded usefulness* alongside novelty, not novelty alone.
3. **Grounded *evaluation* of grounded *generation*.** Use RAG-Novelty / literature-grounded assessment
   to score ideas, avoiding the unreliable bare LLM-judge.
4. **Cost/benefit of grounding.** Outcome-imagination and small-experiments grounding cost compute;
   quantifying accuracy-per-token across mechanisms would be novel and practical.

## 9. Recommendations for Our Experiment

- **Backbone**: `code/hypogenic_chicagohai` (HypoGeniC + HypoRefine + Union). It already ships the
  *ungrounded baseline*, *induction*, and *literature-comparison* conditions on a shared task set with
  shared metrics — minimal glue to add the remaining grounding arms.
- **Datasets**: 3–4 HypoGeniC *real* tasks (deceptive_reviews, headline_binary, dreaddit/AIGC) + 2
  HypoBench *synthetic* controllable tasks (clean ground-truth recovery) + MOOSE-Chem for idea-level
  novelty. All already local in `datasets/` / `code/`.
- **Conditions (the ablation)**: (0) ungrounded zero/few-shot → (1) induction (HypoGeniC) →
  (2) literature-comparison (SciMON-style boosting / HypoRefine) → (3) literature+data Union →
  (4) outcome-imagination/small-experiment grounding (downstream-accuracy or SGA-style check) →
  (5) proposal-writing (full structured proposal + reviewer). Hold model and task fixed.
- **Metrics**: primary = downstream IND/OOD accuracy + HypoBench discovery rate / MOOSE-Chem rediscovery;
  secondary = grounded novelty (RAG-Novelty) + feasibility, with Cohen's κ for any human/LLM judging.
- **Models**: at least one strong API model + one open model (cross-model generalization, per HypoBench).
- **Statistics**: blind/randomized judging where human/LLM scores are used; Welch's t-test + multiple-
  comparison correction; report idea-diversity to catch mode collapse.

#### 1. Can LLMs Generate Novel Research Ideas? A Large-Scale Human Study with 100+ NLP Researchers
- **arXiv / Year**: [2409.04109](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.04109) / 2024  ·  citations≈372  ·  relevance 3/3
- **Authors**: Chenglei Si, Diyi Yang, Tatsunori Hashimoto (Stanford University)
- **Grounding mechanism(s)**: literature-comparison; retrieval; proposal-writing; data-driven
- **Task / setting**: Research ideation in NLP. Input: a fixed natural-language instruction with one of 7 prompting-research topics (Bias, Coding, Safety, Multilinguality, Factuality, Math, Uncertainty), a structured idea template, and a demonstration example. Output: a full project proposal (title, problem statement, motivation, proposed method, step-by-step experiment plan, test case examples, fallback plan). Both human experts and the LLM agent produce ideas in this format; 79 expert reviewers then blind-review them on novelty, excitement, feasibility, expected effectiveness, and overall (1-10 scale). Three conditions compared: Human Ideas, AI Ideas (agent top-ranked), and AI Ideas + Human Rerank (first author manually selected agent's best).
- **Method**: The LLM ideation agent (backbone claude-3-5-sonnet-20240620) has three components. (1) Paper retrieval for RAG: the LLM emits function calls (KeywordQuery, PaperQuery, GetReferences) to the Semantic Scholar API, retrieving up to N=120 papers (top-20 per call), then LLM-scores/reranks them for relevance, empirical nature, and inspiration value, grounding generation in related work. (2) Idea generation: prompt the LLM to overgenerate 4000 seed ideas per topic, using k=6 manually written demonstration examples plus k=10 randomly sampled retrieved papers (titles+abstracts) prepended, plus titles of prior ideas to avoid repetition; dedup via all-MiniLM-L6-v2 cosine similarity threshold 0.8 (leaving ~5% / ~200 unique). (3) Idea ranking: a Claude-3.5 zero-shot pairwise ranker validated on 1200 scraped ICLR 2024 LLM-related submissions (71.4% accuracy at predicting accept/reject), run as an N=5-round Swiss-system tournament. Heavy confounder control: standardized grant-style proposal template, an LLM style-normalization/anonymization module (drove human-vs-AI distinguishability to ~50%), topic-distribution matching, and strict reviewer assignment. Statistical analysis uses three tests (per-review, per-idea, per-reviewer) with Welch's t-tests and Bonferroni correction.
- **Datasets**: 104 total recruited expert participants: 49 idea writers (26 institutions) + 79 reviewers (32 institutions), 24 overlap; 298 unique blind reviews collected (47 ideas x conditions; 119 Human / 109 AI / 109 AI+Rerank review-level datapoints); 147 ideas total: 49 per condition (Human, AI, AI+Human Rerank); 1200 scraped ICLR 2024 LLM-related submissions with review scores/decisions (used to build/validate the ranker and as a review-quality baseline); 4000 LLM seed ideas generated per topic across 7 NLP topics
- **Baselines**: Human Ideas: proposals written by 49 recruited expert NLP researchers (the primary human-expert baseline); AI Ideas: agent's top LLM-ranked proposals; AI Ideas + Human Rerank: agent proposals manually reranked by the first author (upper-bound estimate of AI quality); LLM-as-judge evaluators benchmarked: GPT-4o direct/pairwise, Claude-3.5 direct/pairwise, the 'AI Scientist' (Lu et al. 2024) reviewer; Reviewer-consistency baselines: NeurIPS 2021 (66.0%), ICLR 2024 (71.9%), random (50%)
- **Metrics**: Novelty score (1-10); Excitement score (1-10); Feasibility score (1-10); Expected Effectiveness score (1-10); Overall score (1-10); Free-text rationale per metric; Inter-reviewer balanced-accuracy agreement; Idea-diversity / non-duplicate percentage; LLM-evaluator agreement accuracy; Statistical significance via Welch's t-tests + Bonferroni (3 tests)
- **Key results**: Main finding: AI ideas judged significantly more novel than human ideas, robust across all 3 statistical tests. Test 1 (per-review): Novelty Human 4.84 vs AI 5.64 (p<0.01) vs AI+Rerank 5.81 (p<0.001); Excitement Human 4.55 vs AI 5.19 (p<0.05) vs AI+Rerank 5.46 (p<0.01); AI+Rerank Overall 5.34 > Human 4.68 (p<0.05); Feasibility slightly lower for AI (6.34/6.44 vs 6.61, n.s.). Test 2 (per-idea, N=49): Novelty Human 4.86 vs AI 5.62 (p<0.05) vs AI+Rerank 5.78 (p<0.01). Test 3 (per-reviewer): Novelty mean diff +0.94 (AI, p<0.01) and +0.86 (AI+Rerank, p<0.01). Reviewer-focus: Overall correlates with novelty (r=0.725) and excitement (r=0.854) but ~0 with feasibility (r=0.097). Limitations of LLMs: idea diversity collapses (only ~200 non-duplicates out of 4000 generated seed ideas, plateauing); LLMs cannot evaluate reliably (best Claude-3.5 pairwise 53.3%, below human consistency 56.1%; AI Scientist reviewer 43.3%). Inter-reviewer agreement 56.1% (below NeurIPS'21 66.0% and ICLR'24 71.9%). Human writers self-report their submitted ideas as only top ~43% of their ideas; 80/298 reviews cited existing papers to dispute novelty.
- **Limitations**: Human experts may not submit their best ideas (avg self-rated top 43%; 37/49 ideated on the spot within a 10-day limit), possibly inflating relative AI novelty. Reviewing ideas (vs executed papers) is highly subjective with only 56.1% inter-reviewer agreement. Novelty/feasibility judgments are made on unexecuted proposals, so they may not translate to real research outcomes (motivating a proposed end-to-end execution study). Study underpowered to conclusively detect feasibility/excitement effects. Restricted to prompting-based NLP research and US-based participants. LLM agent lacks idea diversity and cannot self-evaluate, undermining the overgenerate-and-rank paradigm; human reranking was needed for the upper bound.
- **Code**: https://github.com/NoviScl/AI-Researcher
- **Relevance to hypothesis**: Strongly relevant and largely supportive of the grounded-novelty hypothesis. The agent does NOT use abstract/ungrounded prompting: novelty is grounded through (a) literature-comparison/retrieval — RAG over Semantic Scholar retrieves and reranks related work that is fed into generation so ideas are aware of prior art and explicitly avoid repetition; (b) proposal-writing — a structured grant-style template forces detailed, communicable ideas; (c) data-driven grounding — the ranker is calibrated on real ICLR review outcomes. Under this grounding, the LLM's ideas were judged significantly MORE novel than expert humans, providing strong empirical evidence that retrieval/literature-grounded ideation produces high-novelty ideas. Caveats for the hypothesis: the paper does not run an ablation isolating grounded vs ungrounded prompting (no such controlled comparison), and it surfaces that novelty gains came partly at the cost of feasibility and that LLM self-evaluation/diversity remain weak. Notably, reviewers observed human ideas were 'more grounded in existing research and practical considerations but may be less innovative,' a nuance suggesting grounding and raw novelty can trade off. Useful as a CORE reference for the benefit of literature/retrieval grounding and proposal-writing scaffolds in idea generation.

#### 2. SCIMON: Scientific Inspiration Machines Optimized for Novelty
- **arXiv / Year**: [2305.14259](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.14259) / 2023  ·  citations≈182  ·  relevance 3/3
- **Authors**: Qingyun Wang, Doug Downey, Heng Ji, Tom Hope
- **Grounding mechanism(s)**: literature-comparison; retrieval; knowledge-graph; induction; data-driven
- **Task / setting**: Input: a background context B (problems, motivations, experimental settings, constraints) plus an optional seed term v drawn from the ground-truth relation. Output: a single full natural-language sentence describing a novel research idea (e.g., a method used-for a task) that addresses the background and is novel relative to B and the broader literature. This replaces the traditional binary A-B-C link-prediction formulation of literature-based discovery with open-ended, contextual idea generation. Domains: AI/NLP (primary) and a biochemical/PubMed case study.
- **Method**: SCIMON has three components. (1) Inspiration Retrieval: given the background, it retrieves "inspirations" from three sources -- Semantic Neighbors (target terms of semantically similar training problems via SentenceBERT all-mpnet-base-v2 over a fully connected graph), KG Neighbors (one-hop nodes from a global background knowledge graph of used-for relations built by scientific IE), and Citation Neighbors (top cited paper titles by embedding similarity). (2) Generation Module: in-context learning with GPT-3.5/GPT-4 (zero-shot, few-shot random, few-shot retrieval) and fine-tuned T5-large; a novel in-context contrastive InfoNCE objective penalizes copying phrases from the background by using in-context negatives. (3) Iterative Novelty Boosting: a retrieve-compare-update loop that retrieves k=20 nearest prior ideas from a reference corpus, and if cosine similarity exceeds a threshold tau=0.6, instructs the LLM to make the idea significantly different, iterating until all similarities fall below threshold. Training data is auto-collected from papers via scientific IE (PL-Marker/SciERC entities and used-for relations, SciCo coreference, ScispaCy abbreviation resolution, sequential sentence classification to split Background vs target sentences), with temporal train/dev/test splits (<2021/2021/2022) to avoid contamination.
- **Datasets**: NLP corpus: 67,408 ACL Anthology papers from S2ORC (1952-2022); 58,874 before 2021, 5,946 in 2021, 2,588 in 2022; Dataset instances: Train 114,310 / Valid 16,195 / Test 5,309 (forward+backward used-for relations); Gold test subset: 194 high-quality manually filtered instances (low background-target similarity, 10th percentile, cosine <= 0.074); Retrieval scale (NLP): 59k papers, 374k sentences; background KG with 197k nodes and 261k relations; 87k citation-network paper titles; Biochemical corpus: PubMed papers 1988-2024 via Entrez/PubTator3; 4,767 papers before 2023/02, 642 from 2023/02-08, 299 after 2023/08 (5,708 total PubMed)
- **Baselines**: GPT-3.5 (davinci-003) zero-shot (GPT3.5ZS); GPT-3.5 few-shot random (GPT3.5FS); GPT-3.5 few-shot retrieval (GPT3.5Retr); GPT-4 (gpt-4-0314) zero-shot (GPT4ZS) and few-shot (GPT4FS); Fine-tuned T5-large (vanilla); SCIMON variants: T5+CL (contrastive), T5+SN/KG/CT+CL, GPT4FS+SN/KG/CT; Meditron-7b (biochemical base LLM) and +SN/KG/CT variants; Ground-truth ideas from the original papers (upper-bound comparison)
- **Metrics**: Human evaluation: helpful/unhelpful binary rating (relevance, novelty, clarity, reasonableness) - Study I; Human pairwise ranking of technical detail and novelty/incrementality vs. each other and vs. ground truth - Study II; Human evaluation of novelty boosting: % ideas made different and % made more novel; new terms added - Study III; Automated: ROUGE-L, BERTScore (SciBERT), BARTScore; Inter-annotator agreement (%)
- **Key results**: Study I: GPT4FS and GPT4FS+KG dominate (helpful votes 66% and 62%); best non-GPT4 model is T5+SN+CL (48% helpful). GPT-3.5 models underperform fine-tuned T5. Study II: vs. ground truth, in 85% of comparisons the real paper idea has significantly higher technical level and novelty; GPT4FS+KG has higher technical detail in 48% and is less incremental (more novel) in 45% of pairs vs. GPT4FS (rest mostly ties). Study III (novelty boosting): first iteration makes 88.9% of updated ideas substantially different and increases novelty for ~54-56% of ideas (e.g., +54.4% for GPT4FS, +55.6% SN); second iteration adds novelty for 57.8% more; novelty never drops. Automated eval (Table 9, gold subset): fine-tuned T5 variants score highest on ROUGE-L/BERTScore (T5+SN+CL R-L 0.258, BERT 0.686) while GPT-4 outputs score lower (GPT4FS R-L 0.151) despite higher human preference, because GPT-4 outputs are longer and human-preferred; contrastive augmentation (CL) improves over vanilla fine-tuning by reducing copying. Biochemical case study: 80% of generated directions rated positive; evaluators preferred generated outputs over ground truth on technical detail in this domain. IE preprocessing precision 91-100% per step (relation extraction 65%), overall 79.7% pass rate.
- **Limitations**: Generated ideas remain incremental and shallow: models often recombine popular concepts (e.g., adding dynamic/adaptive, graph models, multimodal fusion) and copy/rephrase from the background context; in 85% of cases ground-truth paper ideas are judged significantly more novel and technically deep. Dependent on noisy IE/retrieval components (coreference, abbreviation, relation extraction at 65% precision; SentenceBERT not fine-tuned). Mainly limited to models up to 7B params (plus GPT-3.5/4 API); GPT results may not be reproducible due to API/model changes. Setting uses a seed term taken from ground truth (a no-seed setting would be harder); only English ACL/PubMed papers. Automatic metrics are weak proxies for open-ended generation. Biochemical experiment is preliminary.
- **Code**: https://github.com/eaglew/clbd
- **Relevance to hypothesis**: Strongly supportive and central to the hypothesis. SCIMON operationalizes "grounded novelty": rather than prompting an LLM abstractly for novel ideas, it grounds generation via literature retrieval (semantic-neighbor inspirations = induction/analogy from related solved problems, a knowledge graph, and citation links) and grounds the NOTION of novelty via explicit literature-comparison (the iterative retrieve-compare-update loop that scores similarity to prior work and forces differentiation below a threshold). Human studies show these grounding mechanisms measurably increase novelty (novelty boosting raises novelty for ~54-58% of ideas without ever lowering it) and that KG-grounded inspirations (GPT4FS+KG) yield more technical depth and less incrementality than ungrounded few-shot prompting, while the in-context contrastive objective further curbs ungrounded copying. At the same time, the persistent 85% gap versus real paper ideas tempers the claim, showing grounding helps but does not by itself close the novelty/depth gap to human-generated science.

#### 3. Hypothesis Generation with Large Language Models
- **arXiv / Year**: [2404.04326](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.04326) / 2024  ·  citations≈86  ·  relevance 3/3
- **Authors**: Yangqiaoyu Zhou, Haokun Liu, Tejes Srivastava, Hongyuan Mei, Chenhao Tan
- **Grounding mechanism(s)**: data-driven; induction; small-experiments; literature-comparison; deduction
- **Task / setting**: Data-driven hypothesis generation: input is a set of labeled examples S = {(x_i, y_i)} for a classification task; output is a bank of natural-language hypotheses H = {h_1,...,h_m} describing relationships between x and y. Hypotheses are then used as an interpretable hypothesis-based classifier whose held-out accuracy serves as a proxy for hypothesis quality. The goal is high-quality, interpretable, generalizable hypotheses (not maximizing accuracy per se). Tasks are a synthetic single-hypothesis task plus three challenging social-science prediction tasks.
- **Method**: HypoGeniC (Hypothesis Generation in Context) first prompts an LLM to induce an initial hypothesis bank from a small number of examples (num_init). It then runs an update stage inspired by the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) multi-armed-bandit algorithm: each hypothesis is an 'arm' and each training example a 'pull'. For each training example, the top-k highest-reward hypotheses are selected, used by the LLM to make predictions (a small experiment), and their rewards updated using a UCB formula (accuracy term + exploration term alpha*sqrt(log t / |S_i|)). Examples that too many hypotheses get wrong are stored in a 'wrong example bank'; when it fills (w_max=10), the LLM generates new hypotheses targeting those gaps, and H is pruned to the top-k by reward. At inference, four strategies use the hypotheses (best-accuracy hypothesis, filter-and-weighted-vote, single-step adaptive, two-step adaptive). The key claimed mechanism is LLMs' ability to 'hallucinate' / combine relevant concepts, with the exploration-exploitation reward identifying valuable hypotheses.
- **Datasets**: SHOE SALES (synthetic, 6-class: shoe color must match shirt color; single known valid hypothesis); DECEPTIVE REVIEWS (Ott et al. 2011; 800 genuine + 800 fictitious reviews for 20 Chicago hotels); HEADLINE POPULARITY (Upworthy Research Archive, Matias et al. 2021; binary which-headline-got-more-clicks; built from 150,816 headlines across 22,666 packages); TWEET POPULARITY (Tan et al. 2014; 13,174 topic-and-author-matched tweet pairs, predict more-retweeted); OOD Deceptive Reviews (Li et al. 2013; reviews from four cities/different websites, used as out-of-distribution test)
- **Baselines**: Zero-shot prompting; Few-shot prompting (3 demonstrations); HypoGeniC (no updates) ablation (best initial hypothesis only); Supervised oracle: fine-tuned RoBERTa (200 and 1000 train examples); Supervised oracle: fine-tuned Llama-2-7B (200 and 1000 train examples)

#### 9. MOOSE-Chem: Large Language Models for Rediscovering Unseen Chemistry Scientific Hypotheses
- **arXiv / Year**: [2410.07076](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.07076) / 2024  ·  citations≈59  ·  relevance 3/3
- **Authors**: Zonglin Yang, Wanhao Liu, Ben Gao, Tong Xie, Yuqiang Li, et al. (Erik Cambria, Dongzhan Zhou corresponding)
- **Grounding mechanism(s)**: literature-comparison; deduction; induction; knowledge-graph; multi-agent; retrieval
- **Task / setting**: Input: only a chemistry research background (a research question and/or a background survey) plus a corpus of up to 3,000 chemistry papers (title+abstract) as inspiration space. Output: a list of ranked research hypotheses. The task is to rediscover, without data contamination (LLM trained up to Oct/Dec 2023, papers public only after Jan 2024), the core innovations of high-impact chemistry hypotheses. Decomposed into three subtasks: (1) inspiration retrieval, (2) hypothesis composition from background+inspirations, (3) hypothesis ranking.
- **Method**: The authors propose a fundamental assumption that most chemistry hypotheses can be composed from a research background b and k inspirations (h = f(b, i_1,...,i_k), k in [1,3]), and derive a mathematical decomposition of P(h|b) into a Markov Decision Process over inspiration selection P(i_j|b,h_{j-1},I), state transition P(h_j|b,h_{j-1},i_j), and a ranking function R(h). MOOSE-Chem is a multi-agent LLM framework directly implementing this: (1) an LLM screens literature in windows (default 15 papers, keep 3) to retrieve inspirations that are NOT semantic/citation neighbors of the background (so the association is novel); (2) an evolutionary unit (EU) generates multiple hypothesis mutations, refines each via feedback on validness/novelty/clarity/significance, then recombines them; (3) an LLM rates each hypothesis on validness/novelty/significance/potential, with beam search (default size 15) over up to three rounds. Evaluation uses a reference-based 6-point Matched Score (MS) comparing generated to ground-truth hypotheses, plus expert evaluation by chemistry PhD students.
- **Datasets**: TOMATO-Chem benchmark: 51 high-impact chemistry/material-science papers published and made public only after Jan 2024 (27 Nature/Science, 20 Nature subjournals, 4 other top journals; categories: 21 polymer, 22 organic, 3 inorganic, 5 analytical), each annotated by chemistry PhD students into background, inspirations, hypothesis, experiments, reasoning process; Inspiration corpus of 3,000 most-cited Nature chemistry papers, sub-sampled into corpora of sizes 150, 300, 1000, 3000 (~120 ground-truth inspiration papers included)
- **Baselines**: MOOSE (Yang et al., 2024a) - social science hypothesis discovery with self-refine; SciMON (Wang et al., 2024b) - NLP/biochemical, uses semantic/citation neighbor retrieval; Qi et al. (2024) - biomedical hypothesis discovery via keyword-pertinent retrieval + self-refine; Ablations: MOOSE-Chem w/o multi-step; w/o multi-step & EU
- **Metrics**: Hit Ratio (inspiration retrieval coverage of ground-truth inspirations); Matched Score (MS), 6-point Likert reference-based similarity to ground-truth hypothesis (Top MS, Average MS); Expert (PhD chemist) evaluation of MS; Average Rank Ratio (ranking quality)
- **Key results**: Inspiration retrieval Hit Ratio is surprisingly high: >75% of ground-truth inspirations covered in just the top 4% of a 3000-paper corpus (e.g., 86.9% top-4% at corpus 3000; 83.7% at corpus 300). Smaller screen windows perform better (window 15 keep 3 over 2 rounds = 83.7% vs window 60 = 71.6%). Inspiration-retrieval ability emerges at small model scale then plateaus (Llama-3.1-70B 83.0% vs GPT-4o 83.7% top-4%). On hypothesis composition (Q2) with ground-truth inspirations, many generated hypotheses reach Top MS of 4-5. On the full copilot in-the-wild setting (|I|=300, top 4% selected), MOOSE-Chem reaches Top MS 4.020 / Average MS 2.564, beating MOOSE (2.882/2.464), SciMON (2.549/2.281), and Qi et al. (2.686/2.356). Expert evaluation confirms many rediscovered hypotheses cover two or more main innovations of Nature/Science papers (MS=3 considered already high). Ranking shows more matched ground-truth inspirations correlate with better rank ratio. Authors propose a bold assumption that LLMs may already encode latent scientific knowledge associations unknown to scientists.
- **Limitations**: Relies on the assumption that hypotheses decompose into background + inspirations, which may not hold for all chemistry; LLM-based MS evaluation may be unreliable in chemistry (mitigated by expert checks but still limited). Evaluation is rediscovery of known (2024) hypotheses rather than truly de novo validated discovery; no wet-lab experimental verification of generated hypotheses. Benchmark is small (51 papers) and annotation by PhD students introduces subjectivity. Ranking ability is modest and partly confounded because hypotheses generated without ground-truth inspirations can also be high quality. Mutation step lowers Average MS by producing some low-sense hypotheses.
- **Code**: https://github.com/ZonglinY/MOOSE-Chem
- **Relevance to hypothesis**: Strongly supports the grounded-novelty hypothesis. Rather than prompting an LLM with an abstract/ungrounded request to "be novel," MOOSE-Chem operationally grounds novelty through explicit literature comparison and structured association: it retrieves inspiration papers deliberately chosen to be NON-neighbors of the background (so combining them yields genuinely novel, not redundant, knowledge), grounds composition in a deductive mathematical decomposition (h=f(b,inspirations)) and inductive reasoning from known to unknown knowledge, and uses multi-round literature screening as a knowledge-graph-like association mechanism. The ablations show that the grounding components (multi-step inspiration retrieval and the evolutionary mutation/recombination of background-inspiration associations) substantially raise hypothesis quality (Top MS 2.863 -> 4.020) over ungrounded/simpler baselines, and the framework rediscovers Nature/Science-level innovations. This evidences that grounding the notion of novelty in concrete cross-literature knowledge associations produces better scientific ideas than abstract prompting.

#### 10. LLM and Simulation as Bilevel Optimizers: A New Paradigm to Advance Physical Scientific Discovery
- **arXiv / Year**: [2405.09783](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.09783) / 2024  ·  citations≈85  ·  relevance 3/3
- **Authors**: Pingchuan Ma, Tsun-Hsuan Wang, Minghao Guo, Zhiqing Sun, Joshua B. Tenenbaum, Daniela Rus, Chuang Gan, Wojciech Matusik
- **Grounding mechanism(s)**: small-experiments; outcome-imagination; proposal-writing; deduction; induction
- **Task / setting**: Input: an initial scientific solution (e.g., code for a purely linear/elastic material, or a starting SMILES molecular structure) plus a target observation. Output: a refined discrete symbolic expression E (a constitutive law as Python code, or a molecule SMILES) together with optimized continuous parameters theta (material parameters, or 3D atom coordinates). Two domains: (1) constitutive law discovery — recover symbolic material models + material parameters from a ground-truth particle-position trajectory; (2) molecule design — find molecular structure + 3D conformation matching target quantum-mechanical properties (HOMO, LUMO, HOMO-LUMO gap). The LLM proposes/revises hypotheses; a differentiable simulator executes them and returns loss/observational feedback used to ground and improve subsequent proposals.
- **Method**: SGA is a bilevel optimization framework. The outer level uses an LLM (gpt-4-turbo-preview) as a thinker/proposer that analyzes past simulation results, devises a high-level plan, and emits a new discrete symbolic expression E plus a continuous parameterization theta, generated via an evolutionary search over a top-K heap of best prior solutions. The inner level performs gradient-based optimization of continuous parameters theta via a differentiable simulator (differentiable Material Point Method via Warp for physics; RDKit ETKDG+MMFF conformer generation and a UniMol model fine-tuned on QM9 for molecules), yielding observational feedback and a loss. An exploit-and-explore strategy adjusts the LLM generation temperature (exploit T=0.5, explore T=1.0) to balance refining good candidates vs. discovering new structures. The heap is updated with optimized solutions each iteration, and after a few bilevel iterations the top-1 solution is returned. The same pipeline generalizes across disciplines with only prompt changes.
- **Datasets**: QM9 dataset (Ramakrishnan et al. 2014) used to fine-tune the UniMol property predictor and to normalize quantum-mechanical target values; 8 self-designed tasks: 4 constitutive-law discovery tasks (a non-linear elastic, b von Mises plastic, c granular, d weakly compressible fluid) and 4 molecule-design tasks (e HOMO=0, f LUMO=0, g HOMO-LUMO gap=0, h gap=-2); An invented 'imaginary' constitutive law (50% von Mises plasticity + 30% granular + 20% weakly compressible fluid) constructed to test generalization vs. memorization; Ground-truth particle-position trajectories from differentiable MPM simulation as supervision; SRBench (La Cava et al. 2021) used for symbolic-regression comparison
- **Baselines**: Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting; FunSearch (Romera-Paredes et al. 2023); Eureka (Ma et al. 2024); OPRO / Optimization by PROmpting (Yang et al. 2024); Ours (no bilevel) ablation; Ours (no exploit) ablation; Symbolic regression baselines from SRBench (FFX, MLP, FEAT, DSO, Operon) plus pre-trained SR methods (SymbolicGPT, NeSymReS, T-JSL); GhemGE population-based molecule design (Yoshikawa et al. 2018); Alternative LLM backbones: GPT-3.5, Claude-3-Sonnet, Mixtral-8x7B (vs GPT-4)
- **Metrics**: Optimization loss (mean squared error of fitted trajectory / property to ground truth, lower is better); Symbolic regression R2, MSE, MAE, and whether output is symbolic; Loss-trend / convergence across iterations; Simulation validity (fraction of LLM proposals that are simulatable) for exploit/explore ablation; Backbone-LLM rank comparison; Qualitative domain-expert analysis of discovered laws/molecules
- **Key results**: SGA outperforms all LLM-based baselines (CoT, FunSearch, Eureka, OPRO) by orders of magnitude on the 8 tasks (e.g., constitutive task (a): SGA 5.2e-5 vs CoT 7298.5, FunSearch 210.3, Eureka 7128.0, OPRO 136.2). Removing bilevel optimization (Ours no-bilevel: e.g., 90.2 on task a) sharply degrades performance, confirming simulation grounding is the key driver; removing exploitation also hurts on hard tasks (>50% gap on b and f). On the imaginary constitutive law (anti-memorization test) SGA reaches loss 1.3e-3 vs FunSearch 105.0, Eureka 89.1, OPRO 98.0. In symbolic regression (Table 2) SGA tops SRBench with R2=0.9990, MSE=1.7e4, MAE=8.6e1 under a harder setting. Vs GhemGE on molecule tasks SGA gives much lower loss. GPT-4 is the best backbone but the workflow also works with Claude-3-Sonnet (2nd on most constitutive tasks) and Mixtral-8x7B (tops 2 molecule tasks). Notably SGA proposes solutions 'beyond human expectation yet sensible under analysis by domain experts' (e.g., a quadratic deviatoric term constitutive law; molecules with interpretable electron-withdrawing/non-bonding structures).
- **Limitations**: Requires a domain-specific differentiable simulator/oracle (differentiable MPM, UniMol surrogate) and a valid bootstrapping example to ensure success rate, limiting applicability to domains where such simulators exist. Evaluation is confined to two physical-science domains (constitutive laws, small molecules). Inner-level relies on differentiable simulation and a UniMol property predictor fine-tuned on QM9, so quality is bounded by surrogate accuracy. Search space is highly non-linear with many local optima; results averaged over only 5 seeds. Best results depend on GPT-4 (cost), though open models partially substitute. 'Novelty' of proposals is judged via simulation loss plus post-hoc domain-expert inspection rather than a systematic novelty metric.
- **Code**: —
- **Relevance to hypothesis**: Strongly supports the grounded-novelty hypothesis. SGA shows that LLM-generated scientific hypotheses become far better — and yield novel, expert-validated discoveries 'beyond human expectation' — when each LLM proposal is grounded through actual small experiments: a differentiable simulator executes the hypothesis (proposal-writing as code), returns observational feedback/loss (outcome realized rather than imagined), and that evidence drives the next deductive/inductive refinement. The ablations are the crux: removing the simulation grounding (no-bilevel, i.e., LLM-only abstract optimization) collapses performance by orders of magnitude, directly demonstrating that grounding novelty in experimental feedback beats ungrounded LLM idea generation. The imaginary-law experiment further shows the gains come from grounded search, not memorized priors.

#### 11. ResearchAgent: Iterative Research Idea Generation over Scientific Literature with Large Language Models
- **arXiv / Year**: [2404.07738](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.07738) / 2024  ·  citations≈194  ·  relevance 3/3
- **Authors**: Jinheon Baek, Sujay Kumar Jauhar, Silviu Cucerzan, Sung Ju Hwang
- **Grounding mechanism(s)**: literature-comparison; knowledge-graph; retrieval; multi-agent; proposal-writing; induction
- **Task / setting**: Open-ended research idea generation. Input: a single core scientific paper (high-impact, post-May-2023) from the Semantic Scholar Academic Graph. Output: a structured research idea o = [p, m, d] composed of (1) a problem/research question, (2) a proposed method, and (3) an experiment design. Each is generated sequentially conditioned on the prior steps and the augmenting literature/entities, then iteratively refined.
- **Method**: Starting from a core paper, ResearchAgent gathers related work by traversing the citation graph (selecting references via abstract similarity) to ground ideas in prior literature. It additionally augments the LLM with an entity-centric knowledge store K (a sparse entity co-occurrence matrix built with the BLINK entity linker over 50,091 papers), retrieving top-k relevant external entities not in the input context to enable cross-domain idea cross-pollination. Idea generation is decomposed into three LLM steps: problem identification p=LLM(Tp(L)), method development m=LLM(Tm(p,L)), and experiment design d=LLM(Te(p,m,L)). The ideas are then iteratively refined (up to ~3 effective iterations) by 15 LLM ReviewingAgents (5 criteria per idea component) whose evaluation criteria are induced from human researcher judgments via LLM prompting on 10 annotated idea pairs (human-preference alignment). GPT-4 is the main backbone; Llama3, Mixtral, Qwen1.5, and GPT-3.5 are also tested.
- **Datasets**: Benchmark of 300 high-impact core papers (>20 citations, post-May-2023) sampled from Semantic Scholar Academic Graph; avg 87 reference papers per core paper, avg 2.17 entities per abstract; Entity-centric knowledge store built from 50,091 papers (May 1-Dec 31, 2023) plus references via BLINK entity linker; 10 human-annotated research-idea pairs with 5-point Likert scores (for criteria induction); evaluations by 10 expert researchers (>=3 papers each)
- **Baselines**: Naive ResearchAgent (core paper only); ResearchAgent w/o Entity Retrieval (core paper + citation references, no entities); Ablations: w/o Entities, w/ Random Entities, w/o References, w/ Random References, w/o Entities & References; SciMON (Wang et al., 2023b); Hypothesis Proposer (Yang et al., 2023); Cross-LLM comparison: GPT-4, GPT-3.5, Llama3-8B, Mixtral-8x7B, Qwen1.5-32B
- **Metrics**: Human evaluation (5-point Likert per criterion + pairwise win ratio); Model-based evaluation with GPT-4 (5-point Likert + pairwise); Problem criteria: Clarity, Relevance, Originality, Feasibility, Significance; Method criteria: Clarity, Validity, Rigorousness, Innovativeness, Generalizability; Experiment criteria: Clarity, Validity, Robustness, Feasibility, Reproducibility; Inter-annotator agreement (Spearman's rho, Cohen's kappa); human-model agreement
- **Key results**: Full ResearchAgent outperforms all ablated baselines by large margins on every metric across problem/method/experiment in both human and model evaluations, with especially strong gains on creativity-related metrics (Originality for problems, Innovativeness for methods), attributed to the entity-centric knowledge store surfacing novel cross-domain concepts. Ablation (model-based, problem score): full 4.52 vs w/o Entities 4.35, w/o References 4.26, w/o Entities & References 4.20; random elements help more than nothing. ResearchAgent beats prior hypothesis-generation methods (Table 3): Originality 4.77 vs SciMON 4.56 and Hypothesis Proposer 4.07; Significance 4.81 vs 4.15/4.11. Iterative refinement improves quality but saturates after ~3 iterations. High-impact (more-cited) core papers yield higher-quality ideas. Human-induced criteria alignment makes model-based score distributions resemble human judgments; inter-annotator and human-model agreements are high. Performance degrades substantially with weaker LLMs (Mixtral, GPT-3.5 show marginal benefit from augmentation).
- **Limitations**: Entity-centric knowledge store is built only from titles/abstracts of a limited paper set (cost-constrained), missing many entities and relations; BLINK yields only ~3 entities/paper (limited, open-domain coverage). LLM-based generation may hallucinate; generated ideas are not experimentally validated. ReviewingAgents use only 5 fixed criteria per component (15 agents total), not capturing all domains/perspectives. Less suited to theoretical sciences (math/proof generation) where experiment-design steps are less relevant. No ground-truth idea references exist, so evaluation relies on LLM-as-judge and human ratings. Ethical risks: misuse and unintentional plagiarism via training-data regurgitation.
- **Code**: https://github.com/JinheonBaek/ResearchAgent
- **Relevance to hypothesis**: Strongly SUPPORTS the hypothesis that grounding novelty produces better ideas. The paper's central finding is that grounding LLM idea generation in concrete sources — related literature via a citation graph (literature-comparison) and cross-domain concepts via an entity-centric knowledge store/knowledge graph (induction over accumulated entity co-occurrences + retrieval) — yields substantially more novel/original ideas than the ungrounded Naive baseline that uses only the core paper. The ablation directly isolates grounding: removing entities or references lowers scores, and notably the largest gains appear on creativity metrics (Originality, Innovativeness), exactly the 'novelty' construct the hypothesis targets. It further grounds novelty through proposal-writing (generating full problem/method/experiment proposals) and multi-agent iterative refinement, with reviewing criteria induced from human judgments (induction). The cross-LLM result that grounding helps only capable models, plus the qualitative CRISPR/Drosophila example of entities surfacing non-trivial cross-domain links, reinforces that grounding mechanisms, not abstract prompting, drive novelty. The main caveat relative to the hypothesis: novelty is evaluated via LLM/human ratings rather than literature-checked true novelty or executed small experiments.

#### 12. Chain of Ideas: Revolutionizing Research Via Novel Idea Development with LLM Agents
- **arXiv / Year**: [2410.13185](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.13185) / 2024  ·  citations≈91  ·  relevance 3/3
- **Authors**: Long Li, Weiwen Xu, Jiayan Guo, ... Deli Zhao, Lidong Bing (DAMO Academy, Alibaba Group)
- **Grounding mechanism(s)**: literature-comparison; induction; multi-agent; outcome-imagination; retrieval
- **Task / setting**: Input: a research topic (extracted from a recent paper). The CoI agent retrieves an anchor paper, extends a chain backward (references) and forward (citing works), extracts each paper's idea, and summarizes the evolution/trend between adjacent ideas. Output: (1) a novel research idea grounded in the inferred future trend of that chain, and (2) an associated experiment design / plan to implement the idea. Evaluation is pairwise (idea vs idea) producing ELO rankings across methods.
- **Method**: CoI first retrieves an anchor paper for a topic via Semantic Scholar plus text-embedding-3-large cosine ranking, then constructs a chain by selecting relevant references and subsequent works, extending backward and forward (max chain length 5, 3 branches). It prompts the LLM to extract the idea from each paper, inherit their progressive relations into a Chain-of-Ideas, and summarize the developmental trend by analyzing the evolution between any two adjacent ideas (e.g., CoT to GoT). It then predicts a future trend and generates a new idea grounded in that trajectory (a few-shot, human-practice-like progression), followed by an iterative review-and-refinement loop and an experiment-design stage that uses prior works' experiments as few-shot exemplars. GPT-4o (05-13) is the main LLM, GPT-4o-mini summarizes full papers. The authors also build Idea Arena, a Round-Robin pairwise ELO evaluation using LLM judges and human evaluators.
- **Datasets**: 50 research topics for evaluation, extracted by GPT-4o from recent papers selected by 10 researchers from HuggingFace Daily Papers (submitted Aug 1 - Sep 15, 2024, after the LLM data cutoff); each topic includes the proposed idea and experiment design used as the 'Real Paper' baseline
- **Baselines**: RAG (vanilla retrieval-augmented generation research agent); ResearchAgent (Baek et al., 2024) - knowledge-graph retrieval + multi-agent peer-discussion refinement; GPT-Researcher (Assafelovic, 2023) - plan-and-solve + RAG agent; AI-Scientist (Lu et al., 2024) - idea-generation/experiment-design components; Real Paper (human-written ideas/experiment designs from the source papers)
- **Metrics**: ELO scores via Idea Arena pairwise Round-Robin tournament; Idea criteria: Novelty, Significance, Clarity, Feasibility, Expected Effectiveness; Experiment-design criteria: Feasibility, Technical Quality, Clarity; Human-model agreement rate (e.g., 70.8% avg between GPT-4o judge and humans); Human pairwise evaluation
- **Key results**: CoI agent consistently ranks first among all automated baselines in Idea Arena, surpassing the second-best method by 56 ELO points in human evaluation, and generates ideas judged as novel as those of human experts (Real Paper). GPT-4o shows ~70.8% average agreement with human judges (highest among GPT-4o, Gemini-1.5-Pro, Claude-3.5-Sonnet), validating Idea Arena. Ablations show that the CoI construction and explicit developmental-trend analysis are the most important components, and that a clear developmental-trend analysis is more pivotal for novelty than the sheer quantity of related literature; removing the trend/future-trend modules degrades novelty and effectiveness (the LLM produces more abstract ideas).
- **Limitations**: Evaluation is limited to 50 AI-domain topics due to high generation/evaluation cost; relies on an LLM judge (GPT-4o) for most analysis, which only moderately agrees (~70%) with humans on individual criteria; idea quality depends on the anchor-paper retrieval and citation network coverage; chain length and branch count are fixed hyperparameters; experiments confined to the AI field, leaving cross-domain generalization untested.
- **Code**: https://github.com/DAMO-NLP-SG/CoI-Agent
- **Relevance to hypothesis**: Strongly supports the hypothesis. The paper's central thesis is that dumping an undifferentiated mass of retrieved literature into an LLM (an essentially ungrounded prompt) yields incoherent, pseudo-novel ideas, whereas grounding novelty in the field's actual developmental trajectory yields better ideas. CoI operationalizes grounding via literature-comparison and inductive trend inference: it chains papers chronologically, induces the evolution between adjacent ideas, imagines the future trend, and only then proposes a novel idea aligned with that trend. The 56-ELO human-eval gain over the best ungrounded/less-grounded baseline, plus ablations showing trend analysis matters more than literature quantity, are direct evidence that grounding the notion of novelty (here through literature comparison and induction of research trends) produces more novel and effective ideas than abstract retrieval-then-prompt approaches.

#### 13. Nova: An Iterative Planning and Search Approach to Enhance Novelty and Diversity of LLM Generated Ideas

- **Authors**: Xiang Hu, Hongyu Fu, Jinge Wang, Yifeng Wang, Zhikun Li, et al. (Lili Pan, Zhenzhong Lan, corresponding)
- **Grounding mechanism(s)**: literature-comparison; retrieval; proposal-writing; deduction; induction; knowledge-graph; data-driven
- **Task / setting**: Input: a single seed paper (from top ML/NLP/CV conferences). The system generates novel and diverse research ideas; novelty and diversity are then automatically and human-evaluated.
- **Method**: Nova is a three-stage pipeline: (1) Initial seed idea generation, which prompts the LLM with the seed paper plus its references, a knowledge-tracking module that surfaces influential recent papers (via social/GitHub engagement metrics), and 10 fundamental scientific-discovery methods drawn from Kuhn's paradigm theory, Laudan's problem-solving model and Nichols's problem-generation theory (e.g., identifying anomalies, exploring theoretical boundaries, integrating interdisciplinary knowledge). - **Metrics**: Swiss System Tournament quality score (Claude-3.5-Sonnet zero-shot pairwise ranker, 5 rounds); Novelty (LLM judges vs top-10 relevant retrieved papers; all-MiniLM-L6-v2 cosine similarity threshold 0.3); Diversity / Non-Duplicate Percentage (proportion of unique ideas, duplication threshold 0.8); Human expert evaluation of novelty and overall quality (10 PhD/professor experts)

Over 80% of Nova ideas are unique (Non-Duplicate Percentage). In human evaluation Nova contributes 37.5% of the top-4 ideas (highest) and only 17.53% of the worst-4 for overall quality, with a similar pattern for novelty; human and automatic rankings agree (Nova > AI-Scientist > ResearchAgent > AI-Researcher). Ablation: removing planning stalls unique-idea growth at step 3 (42.4 -> 44.1), and removing both planning and retrieval stagnates (30.6 -> 31.35), showing both retrieval and planning are needed to keep generating novel ideas.
- **Limitations**: Limited iteration steps: novelty/diversity gains do not continue beyond 3 rounds of iteration. Planning without rewards: the planner uses only the LLM's internal knowledge to form search plans (no reward function), which may limit planning effectiveness; future work plans to add a reward function. Ethics section also flags idea homogenization and quality-control concerns of AI-generated ideas.
- **Code**: —
- **Relevance to hypothesis**: Strongly supports the hypothesis. Nova explicitly grounds novelty rather than relying on abstract prompts: it (a) plans and retrieves external literature each iteration so new ideas are anchored to up-to-date papers (literature-comparison/retrieval), (b) injects 10 structured scientific-discovery methods (Kuhn/Laudan-style anomaly detection, boundary exploration, interdisciplinary integration) to deductively/inductively derive new problems, and (c) expands ideas into full proposals (problem, method, experiment plan) which is a proposal-writing grounding step. The ablation directly demonstrates that grounding the notion of novelty through planning + retrieval is what drives the increase in unique novel ideas; removing these grounding mechanisms causes novel-idea growth to stagnate, evidence that grounded novelty beats ungrounded generation.

#### 14. Scideator: Human-LLM Compound System for Scientific Ideation through Facet Recombination and Novelty Evaluation
- **arXiv / Year**: [2409.14634](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.14634) / 2026  ·  citations≈43  ·  relevance 3/3
- **Authors**: Marissa Radensky, Simra Shahid, Raymond Fok, Pao Siangliulue, Tom Hope, Daniel S. Weld
- **Grounding mechanism(s)**: literature-comparison; retrieval; induction; multi-agent
- **Task / setting**: Input: user-provided seed papers plus an optional ideation topic. The system extracts purpose/mechanism/evaluation facets, retrieves analogous papers at controlled conceptual distances (very near, near, far, very far) from Semantic Scholar, recombines facets via analogy into candidate research ideas, and then classifies each generated idea as "novel" or "not novel" against retrieved literature with a rationale citing specific papers, plus facet-swap suggestions to improve novelty. Module 3 (Idea Novelty Verification) runs a four-step retrieve-then-rerank pipeline: keyword + snippet retrieval, SPECTER embedding filtering (top 100), facet-based RankGPT re-ranking by facet overlap (top 10), and LLM classification using expert-labeled in-context examples encoding a facet-based novelty definition, then suggests three facet-swap alternatives if "not novel." The human steers each stage via facet-level actions.
- **Datasets**: Expert annotation set: 51 ideas annotated (46 generated by Scideator, 5 adapted from OpenReview); 67 consensus-labeled examples total (39 novel, 28 not novel) split into training pool and held-out test set; Classifier ablation held-out test set: 43 ideas (26 novel, 17 not novel); Retrieval ablation test set: 58 'not novel' ideas with known overlapping prior work (13 not-novel held-out instances + 45 published NLP papers); User study: 22 computer-science researchers (16 PhD, 5 master's, 1 industry), HCI and NLP ideation topics
- **Baselines**: Paper-level interaction baseline tool (same backbone LLM, no facet-based modules; users select papers and give free-text instructions); Retrieval: Relevance RankGPT (general-relevance re-ranking), Embedding Filtering only, Snippet Retrieval only, Keyword Retrieval only; Classifier: zero-shot, AI Scientist novelty prompt, OpenReview in-context examples
- **Metrics**: Creativity Support Index (CSI, 0-100); Wilcoxon signed-rank / sign tests; Novelty classification accuracy; Macro F1; Retrieval accuracy (finding overlapping papers in top-10); Inter-annotator agreement (Cohen's kappa); Qualitative thematic analysis of interviews
- **Key results**: User study: Scideator gave significantly more creativity support than the baseline (CSI median 70.5 vs 61.0, Wilcoxon p<.01), strongest in exploration and expressiveness. All 6 treatment participants who found new concepts attributed them to the tool's facets/ideas vs only 2 of 8 in baseline (who cited input papers). Retrieval ablation: complete facet-based system reached 89.66% accuracy at surfacing overlapping papers vs <=13.79% for all ablated variants (Relevance RankGPT 13.79%, Embedding 10.34%, Snippet 8.62%, Keyword 5.17%). Classifier ablation: facet-grounded expert ICL with reasoning reached 0.81 accuracy / 0.79 macro F1, vs zero-shot 0.68/0.65, AI Scientist prompt 0.47/0.44, OpenReview ICL 0.59/0.43. Novelty checker did not significantly raise overall confidence but participants lowered assessments most when an idea was classified "not novel" (verifiable against retrieved papers).
- **Limitations**: Within-subjects user study is small (N=22) with short 20-minute sessions and limited to CS (HCI/NLP) researchers; only 17 participants completed the novelty task with the intended setup (API issues affected several). A clear novelty-feasibility trade-off is confirmed: increasing the novelty steer weight from 1.0 to 4.0 raises novelty 6.4->7.3 but lowers feasibility 6.1->4.9. Human evaluation corroborates automatic scores with high correlation (Pearson r: N 0.982, F 0.948, E 0.716, Overall 0.871).

The interpretability of the learned dimensional-controller adjustments remains an open question. Scope is limited to ML-related domains (ICLR/NeurIPS papers). Novelty/feasibility/effectiveness labels for 2024 papers and feasibility/effectiveness throughout rely on Llama3-prompted scores rather than human ratings, introducing potential proxy-metric noise. Evaluation depends partly on LLM-as-judge (GPT-4), though validated against 30-paper human study.

- **Limitations**: Retrieval was conducted exclusively via the arXiv API restricted to cs.CL, so peer-reviewed and non-CL venues may be under-covered and results are mostly preprints; search-term and manual-curation choices introduce selection bias. The survey provides no quantitative meta-analysis, no new benchmark, and no head-to-head empirical comparison of grounded vs ungrounded generation. It notes unresolved field-wide challenges: hallucination/factual inaccuracy, black-box interpretability, training-data bias, high compute cost, domain-adaptation overfitting, low inter-rater reliability in expert evaluation, and the inadequacy of standard metrics (BLEU/ROUGE) for capturing scientific novelty and merit.

The survey explicitly frames hypothesis quality improvement around grounding the notion of novelty: it dedicates analysis to "novelty boosting and structured reasoning," and contrasts simple/abstract direct prompting (whose output quality depends heavily on prompt clarity) with grounded techniques. Concrete grounding mechanisms it highlights as improving hypothesis quality and reliability include knowledge-graph grounding (KG-CoI, causal graphs), retrieval-augmented generation against external scientific databases, literature-based discovery that surfaces cross-domain connections, multi-agent debate/peer-review, and chain-of-thought/rationale-tracing reasoning. Crucially for the hypothesis, it argues novelty should be evaluated against grounded references (semantic distance from existing publications, citation graphs, ideation chains, emerging-literature alignment) rather than abstractly, i.e., novelty is operationalized through literature comparison. This directly aligns with the claim that grounding novelty (via literature comparison, knowledge graphs, structured reasoning) yields better-grounded and more innovative scientific ideas than ungrounded abstract prompting.
